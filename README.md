## 📌 Project Overview
As Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT become deeply integrated into academic workflows, understanding how students interact with and evaluate AI-generated information is crucial. This research project investigates a fundamental trade-off in Human-AI collaboration: **Scientificity** (academic rigor, precise terminology, and citation of sources) versus **Explainability** (intuitive, comprehensible results using tangible examples). 

Building upon existing literature identifying a gap in why students prioritize academic precision over clarity, this study uses an empirical, within-subject experimental setup to analyze how academic backgrounds influence student preferences when using AI tools.

## 🔬 Research Methodology & Experimental Setup
The study utilizes an explorative survey design to address the core research question: *How do scientifically accurate and clearly explained responses from ChatGPT affect human decision-making during interactions?*

### Variables
* **Independent Variable:** The type of answer provided by ChatGPT (tailored for high explainability vs. high scientificity) alongside student demographic data (age, gender, major, and frequency of AI usage).
* **Dependent Variable:** The explicit preference of students for scientific answers versus explainable answers.

### Participant Breakdown
The experiment collected data from **30 university students** across three distinct academic domains:
* **STEM** (Engineering, Computer Science) – 10 students
* **Social Sciences** (Sociology, Philosophy, Political Science) – 10 students
* **Economics** – 10 students

### The Experiment
Participants were given 6 general-knowledge control questions (generating 12 distinct paired responses from ChatGPT). For each question, ChatGPT generated one answer optimized for **Explainability** and one for **Scientificity** (e.g., explaining the expansion of the universe using metric space stretching vs. General Relativity FLRW metrics). Students selected their preferred text, provided qualitative reasoning, and ranked the criteria in order of importance.

---

## 📊 Key Findings
Statistical analysis was conducted using a two-tailed t-Student distribution to evaluate the null hypothesis ($H_0: \mu = 0$, indicating equal preference).

* **Domain-Specific Variations:** 
  * **Social Sciences & STEM** students demonstrated a statistically significant preference for **scientificity** over explainability.
  * **Economics** students leaned slightly toward **explainability**, though this subset's result was not statistically significant.
* **The Expertise Effect:** For STEM and Social Science tracks, the preference for technical scientificity spiked dramatically when the question fell directly within their own field of study. When evaluating topics outside their expertise, they favored simpler, example-driven explainability.
* **Qualitative Insight:** Open-ended feedback highlighted that while complex answers are valued for their depth where domain familiarity exists, "less scientific approaches with tangible examples" are universally preferred when venturing into unfamiliar technical territory.
